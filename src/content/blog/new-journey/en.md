# New Journey

True generosity toward the future lies in giving all to the present.      —Albert Camus

---
# The Past

:::quote
Memories recede like the tide,

Leaving me alone on the shores of regret,

Staring blankly,

Weeping.
:::

There's a line I've always loved: "Memory always gives meaning to the past." I first came across it during a Chinese language test back in high school — a narrative essay so vivid and honest that the author's memory of their past felt almost tangible. Back then, young and naive, I tried to grasp it with half-formed thoughts, imagining the joys and sorrows of an older me looking back on a life fully lived. In those days, I liked to think that no matter how life's road twisted — smooth or rough — the person I'd become would look back not with nostalgia's ache but with something closer to relief, having shed every title and burden along the way. Every success and failure, every gain and loss, every moment of hope and despair, every joy and pain — they'd all be part of my treasure. And when I finally turned around to face it all, I'd be able to say: it was worth it. I was here.

Just as the past finds its meaning in remembrance, memory is the bridge that connects what was, what is, and what is yet to come. The past feels whole only when we revisit it; the future shifts because of the stories we carry forward. Maybe time isn't a straight line at all. Maybe we think we're walking forward through the days, but we're really just gathering fragments from the folds of memory.

People grow sentimental with age. I often listen to my parents' generation talk about their younger years — the way their eyes light up when they speak of those faded, sepia-toned days, as if time had never really slipped away. And I'd think to myself: memory is a strange and wonderful thing. It travels across rivers of time. It weathers the slow erosion of years. And yet, it feels so ordinary — like a quiet, gentle stream.

But for me, memory has become something I can hardly reach anymore.

I was chatting with a friend the other day when they brought up something from three years ago. Their face lit up as they recounted every detail — and there I was, searching my mind frantically, finding nothing but fog. A blurry haze where a world once stood, leaving me lost and hollow. I hate this kind of foggy memory. The feeling of something just out of reach — like staring into an abyss, powerless.

This isn't an isolated case. I've long known I'm a forgetful person — I often joke with friends that my memory span is seven seconds, like a goldfish. Maybe it's because I've always crammed my life too full, stuffing every gap with one thing after another, never learning how to truly inhabit the present. The days roar past in a blur, and I'm too busy rushing forward to stop and take in the view along the way. So when it comes down to it, remembering has become a luxury.

The past has slipped through my fingers like water. I used to believe my memories would grow rich and full with time — but instead, they've become my greatest regret.

And so I began to fear — fear that the future version of me would sink into even deeper regret, haunted by the blur of a life half-remembered. I had to do something to save the soul I felt slowly slipping away.

This is where the idea for this blog came from. May this small corner of the web hold on to the memories that are quietly fading from my grasp.

# The Present

:::quote
From tomorrow on, I will be a happy person.
Grooming, chopping, and traveling all over the world.
From tomorrow on, I will care for food and vegetables.
I have a house, towards the sea, with spring blossoms.

—Hai Zi
:::

I've started learning to pack my bags — not the kind you take on a trip, but the kind that holds the moments happening right now.

A year ago, I tried journaling. I'd stay up late into the night, jotting down the day's fragments. But most of those entries never made it past the first few lines — like letters I never sent, quietly collecting dust on a shelf. I kept telling myself, "I'll catch up tomorrow." But tomorrow piled onto tomorrow, and the diary fell silent. The words never caught up with today.

This blog is the little house I've built for myself. It doesn't need to be beautiful. It doesn't need to be seen by many. It just needs to be quiet enough — a place where, after the noise has faded, I can sit down and have a conversation with the person I am today.

I've started paying attention to the moments I used to overlook: the first thread of morning light slipping through the curtains, the delicate patter of rain on the windowsill, the crisp clatter of keys in the quiet of the night. I want to capture these feelings in words, so that someday, a future me can follow the breadcrumbs and piece together what it really meant to be alive in this moment.

It isn't easy. When you've spent your whole life running, coming to a sudden stop can make you dizzy. I often sit staring at a blank editor, unsure how to describe what a stretch of time felt like. But I believe that as long as I start writing, I can slowly fill in the pale, empty gaps my memory has left behind.

But why a blog, of all things?

Because I believe blogging itself is a kind of ritual. Choosing a theme, tweaking the colors, setting up the structure, writing the very first line — the whole process feels like building your own world. Every detail of this site carries a little piece of my stubbornness and my taste. And that, in itself, is a form of expression.

Give life to time, not time to life. May I stop trying to fill every minute and instead make every minute worth remembering. May I give all of myself to each breath of the present.

---

# The Future

:::quote
Keep moving forward. Don't look back.
:::

I don't have much of a plan for the future. I want to keep it open — like a roll of unexposed film, waiting for the light to paint on it.

I hope this space will walk with me, year after year. Ten years from now, twenty years from now, when I open these pages again, I hope I'll smile to myself — So that's who I was back then. Those days weren't lived in vain.

Maybe one day, standing somewhere higher, I'll look back at this moment and feel grateful — grateful for the decision I made tonight, for the version of me who sat down in the dark and typed the very first character.

This world is big — big enough to swallow a single voice without a trace. And it's also small — small enough that one small room can hold an entire life.

So I'll just keep writing. Give today to the present. Leave the memories for the future.

Keep moving forward. Don't look back.

**The new journey begins.**
